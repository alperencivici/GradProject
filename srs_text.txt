Kırsof: Digital Marketplace for Local Farmers – Project Proposal 
 
1. Introduction 
This project proposes Kırsof, a web and mobile based C2C (customer-to-customer) 
digital marketplace platform that will connect small-scale local farmers in Turkey 
directly with urban consumers. The primary purpose is to eliminate intermediaries in 
the agricultural supply chain, thereby increasing farmers’ income and lowering 
consumer prices. By leveraging technology to build a “farm-to-table” bridge, Kırsof 
will enable farmers to reach a broader customer base directly, giving them more 
control over pricing, while consumers gain access to fresher and more affordable 
products. The platform also aims to support the digital transformation of the 
agricultural sector by providing an easy-to-use online marketplace for farmers, 
including tools for creating profiles, showcasing products, receiving customer 
feedback, and conducting sales through legally compliant channels. In summary, 
Kırsof’s goal is to empower small farmers, reduce intermediary costs, and ensure 
consumers have transparent access to fresh, reliable local food. 
1.1 Purpose 
The purpose of this graduation project is to design and develop the Kırsof platform 
as a proof-of-concept solution addressing inefficiencies in the current agri-food 
supply chain. It is intended to solve the problem of local farmers having limited 
market access and diminished earnings due to dependence on middlemen. In 
traditional systems, farmers rely on brokers who take a share of profits and drive up 
consumer prices. Kırsof will provide farmers with a digital marketplace to sell 
directly to end consumers, thus mitigating income losses and high consumer costs 
caused by intermediaries. This direct-sale model is expected to strengthen the local 
rural economy and improve farmers’ livelihoods. Additionally, by integrating user-
friendly web/mobile technology, the project intends to increase technological literacy 
among farmers and include them in e-commerce, bridging the digital divide in rural 
areas. Another key purpose is to ensure legal compliance for informal producers – 
Kırsof plans to handle tax requirements (through automated withholding tax on each 
sale) so that even unregistered farmers can sell legally without needing full tax

registration. Overall, the project is aimed at creating a transparent, efficient 
marketplace that benefits both producers and consumers, aligning with broader 
goals of sustainable agriculture and equitable food distribution. 
1.2 Project Scope 
The scope of Kırsof includes the end-to-end development of a multi-platform 
marketplace system and all core functionality needed for buyers and sellers to 
transact. Specifically, the project will deliver: 
• 
A React-based Web Application: a responsive single-page web app for 
desktop and mobile browsers where users can browse products, manage their 
accounts, and place orders. 
• 
A React Native Mobile Application: a cross-platform smartphone app 
(Android 8.0+/iOS 13+) for on-the-go access, including native features like 
camera integration and NFC for payment simulation. 
• 
Backend Server and Database: a cloud-hosted Node.js/Express API server 
and a PostgreSQL database (using Supabase BaaS), providing RESTful 
endpoints, business logic, and persistent data storage. The backend will 
handle authentication, product and order management, payment simulation, 
and integration with external services (e.g., mapping, notifications). 
• 
Core Marketplace Functions: All essential marketplace features are within 
scope. This includes farmer profile pages where sellers can list products (with 
photos, descriptions, and prices), manage inventory, and showcase 
reviews; product catalog browsing with search filters and interactive map 
discovery of nearby producers; a shopping cart allowing customers to add 
items from multiple farmers in one order; an order checkout workflow with 
selection of delivery options and payment processing simulation; and user 
feedback mechanisms including ratings and reviews for products and sellers. 
• 
Regulatory Compliance Module: Kırsof will implement an expense note 
(müstahsil makbuzu) and withholding tax (stopaj) system for every sale, 
automatically deducting the required tax percentage and recording it for 
reporting to tax authorities. This feature is in scope to ensure that sales on the 
platform are legally compliant, enabling farmers without tax IDs to participate 
in the formal economy.

• 
AI-Powered Support Chatbot: An AI chatbot (using a cloud NLP service) will

Push Notifications and Messaging: The system will include a basic

certifications (e.g., PCI-DSS compliance is not pursued since real payments are not 
handled). These will be noted as future work. Within the defined scope, the team will 
ensure that the Kırsof prototype encompasses a fully functional marketplace 
experience (from user registration to order completion) demonstrating the feasibility 
of the concept. 
1.3 Innovative Aspect of the Project 
Kırsof distinguishes itself through several innovative features and approaches that 
are not commonly found together in existing farmer-to-consumer platforms. The 
planned innovations include: 
• 
Integrated Tax Compliance for Informal Producers: Unlike typical 
marketplaces that leave tax matters to users, Kırsof will automatically handle 
withholding tax on each sale. The platform generates an expense note and 
deducts the required stopaj tax from the payment, on behalf of small-scale 
farmers who aren’t registered taxpayers. This approach is novel in our 
country’s context – competing platforms do not yet offer a built-in solution for

barriers for farmers, effectively bringing unregistered sellers into the formal

•

stack is designed to provide a seamless experience on both web and mobile

innovations align with Kırsof’s mission to empower farmers, support the local 
economy, and tightly integrate modern technology with the agricultural sector. By 
implementing these forward-thinking aspects, the project aims to create a model 
platform that demonstrates how digital tools can revolutionize farm-to-table 
commerce in Turkey.

2. Literature Review 
To position Kırsof in context, we reviewed current literature and existing solutions 
related to digital farmer-to-consumer marketplaces both globally and in Turkey. This 
analysis informs how our project will build on prior approaches while filling 
identified gaps. 
  
Global Farmer-to-Consumer Marketplaces: Around the world, digital platforms are 
revolutionizing agricultural supply chains. For example, India’s eNAM (Electronic 
National Agriculture Market) and China’s Pinduoduo are frequently cited as 
successful models connecting farmers directly with buyers. These platforms 
demonstrate advantages like broader market access for farmers, transparent pricing,

and rapid payment processing by cutting out traditional middlemen. Pinduoduo in 
particular introduced an innovative group-buying model allowing consumers to 
collectively purchase from farmers, achieving cost savings and logistics efficiency. 
The success of these initiatives shows that technology can markedly increase 
efficiency and connectivity in agriculture. Academic studies further support this 
trend: for instance, a review by Tajammul et al. (2025) found that agricultural e-
commerce platforms improve farmers’ profit margins and overall supply chain 
efficiency, largely by bypassing brokers and giving farmers direct market entry. This 
not only boosts producer welfare but can also lower prices for consumers. 
Additionally, digital platforms can reduce food waste and post-harvest losses by 
speeding up logistics and enabling demand-driven production planning. These 
findings justify Kırsof’s core premise that a digital marketplace can enhance 
outcomes for both producers and consumers. 
  
Turkish Context and Existing Initiatives: In Turkey, several efforts have emerged to 
connect producers with consumers through technology, though none exactly mirror 
Kırsof’s approach. The Dijital Tarım Pazarı (DİTAP), backed by the government, 
aims to facilitate fair trade of agricultural products by bringing producers and buyers 
together on a digital platform. DİTAP focuses on transparency and wide reach, 
though currently it is more B2B-oriented (farmers to businesses) and thus somewhat 
different from Kırsof’s C2C vision. In the private sector and civil society, various 
models exist: ÇiftçidenEve is a digital marketplace delivering natural and organic 
foods directly from producers to urban consumers, emphasizing additive-free, high-
nutrition products and fair transactions without intermediaries. Likewise, platforms 
such as Kadınların Elinden and Kadın Üreticiden support local production by 
selling goods from women’s cooperatives, leveraging digital channels to increase 
market access for disadvantaged rural women. These social enterprise models have 
shown the potential of e-commerce to uplift specific groups (in this case, women 
producers) by opening new markets. Another notable initiative is Tarlamvar, which 
diverges from typical marketplaces by allowing consumers to “adopt” a fruit tree – 
the farmer cares for the tree and the harvest is delivered to the consumer. This 
creative approach ensures a committed buyer and supports farmers through 
guaranteed sales. Additionally, community-driven marketplaces 
like Good4Trust and Hep Yerinden connect conscious consumers with fair 
producers, often focusing on organic and local products and building a community 
around ethical consumption.

From these examples, we learn that there is a rising movement in Turkey towards

3. Overall Description 
3.1 Product Perspective 
Kırsof is envisioned as a “local farmer’s market” in digital form, positioned in the 
agricultural e-commerce sector as a specialized C2C marketplace. In the broader e-
commerce landscape, most platforms rely on large retailers or already-registered 
businesses selling products online. By contrast, Kırsof focuses exclusively on 
individual small-scale farmers as sellers, carving out a unique niche. In this regard, 
Kırsof will function similarly to an eBay-style C2C platform but dedicated to farm 
products – something not provided by generic Turkish C2C sites like Sahibinden or 
Letgo. Those general platforms lack features to support agricultural sales (e.g. 
handling of fresh produce delivery or farm-specific regulatory compliance). Kırsof 
fills this gap by being tailored specifically to local agriculture and food, including

When compared to emerging farm-to-consumer services in Turkey, Kırsof 
differentiates itself through scope, technology, and business model. Notably, 
platforms such as Good4Trust only accept ecological or organic producers, limiting 
their community to a niche market. Kırsof, however, aims to include all local food 
producers – organic or not – thereby appealing to a broader user base of farmers

simple websites. Kırsof plans to offer a more advanced, integrated system: a 
dedicated mobile app, a map-based discovery feature, and AI-driven support –

technological integration should provide a smoother and richer user experience, 
making Kırsof stand out. Furthermore, Kırsof’s legal-financial model is innovative: 
the platform’s built-in withholding tax and expense note mechanism shifts the tax 
compliance burden from individual farmers to the system itself. For example, on

not only sets Kırsof apart but also provides farmers with peace of mind and 
legitimacy in their sales. 
  
It’s also important to clarify Kırsof’s position relative to major e-commerce players in

sellers and nationwide retail logistics. While they have recently added sub-categories 
for local produce (so-called “village market” sections), they struggle with tax issues 
and product freshness over long distances. Kırsof is not designed to compete head-
on with those platforms, but rather to complement the ecosystem by focusing 
entirely on local, small-scale producers and short-range delivery. By enforcing a 
maximum one-day delivery range and limiting service to specific regions, Kırsof can 
ensure produce is delivered fresh without requiring a complex cold chain, 
addressing the freshness problems larger platforms face. This hyper-local micro-
logistics approach differentiates Kırsof’s operational model and makes it more 
feasible for selling perishable farm goods.

description, stock), or mark items as out of stock. Simple analytics like a 
monthly sales chart or total sales volume may be shown on the store page for 
the farmer’s reference. Importantly, customer feedback will be visible on 
these pages: the farmer’s profile will show an average rating and allow 
visitors to read reviews left by customers, contributing to transparency and 
trust. In essence, the farmer’s store page consolidates their offerings and 
reputation, much like a personal shop.

information including the product name, a description of the item (e.g., origin, 
how it’s produced), price per unit (kg, liter, etc.), current stock quantity, and

Delivery Method Selection: During the checkout process, customers will 
select a delivery option for the products from each farmer in their cart. The 
platform is designed to accommodate four delivery methods: 
1. Pickup from Farm: The buyer will physically go to the farmer’s address 
to collect the product. In this case, no shipping fee is charged. The 
system will share the farmer’s address and contact info with the buyer, 
and both parties can coordinate via the platform messaging if needed. 
This method appeals to nearby customers and avoids shipping entirely. 
2. Local Courier via Kırsof Logistics: A same-day delivery service using 
local couriers partnering with Kırsof. The system will estimate a courier 
fee (likely based on distance or a flat fee within a region) and add it to 
the order. The concept is that a courier can pick up the product from 
the farm and deliver to the buyer typically within hours. (In the 
prototype phase, this is conceptual; the app will allow selection but 
actual courier operations may be simulated or limited to a pilot area.) 
3. Farmer’s Own Shipping (Cargo): The farmer ships the product via a 
standard cargo/postal service (e.g., PTT or private cargo). Each farmer 
can define a flat shipping fee for their products (e.g., 20 TL per order). If 
the customer chooses this, the predetermined shipping cost is applied. 
The farmer will then package and send via their chosen carrier. The 
system will provide a way for the farmer to enter a tracking number, 
which is visible to the customer for order tracking. 
4. Local Area Restriction (Delivery ≤1 Day Distance): As a business rule, 
Kırsof will limit the service radius to ensure deliveries can be 
completed within 24 hours. If a buyer and seller are too far apart (e.g., 
different regions), the platform may prevent the order or warn that 
delivery isn’t available due to distance. This is implemented to 
maintain freshness and feasibility without refrigerated shipping. In 
practice, for the pilot, we assume all users are within a defined region 
(e.g., within Ankara province) so this rule ensures we stick to “local” 
orders.

and their relationships (in an object-oriented design sense). This helps ensure our 
design is consistent and extensible. The fundamental classes (entities) in the Kırsof 
domain and their planned attributes are as follows:

• 
User: This is a base class for all users of the platform. Key attributes include 
userID, name, email, password (hashed), registration date, and role type 
(Visitor, Customer, Farmer, Moderator, Admin). Depending on the role, 
additional fields are present or certain fields may be unused. For instance, if 
the role is Farmer, the user object will have extra attributes such as farm name, 
address, location coordinates (latitude/longitude), and an approval status (to 
indicate if the farmer’s account is verified by an admin). This design allows 
common behaviors (login, profile update) to be handled generally, while 
specifics like farm info are only for farmers. 
• 
Product: Represents an item that a farmer is selling. Attributes include 
productID, name, description, category, price, unit (e.g., “per kg”, “per liter”), 
and current stock quantity. We also store the farmerID of who listed it (linking 
to a User in farmer role). Products can have a status (active or inactive) – 
inactive could mean out-of-stock or removed. Methods associated with 
Product might include adjusting stock (e.g., decrementing stock when sold) 
and updating details. There is also an association to media (photos/videos), 
which we handle by storing file URLs in a separate image table or as part of 
the product attributes in the database (e.g., an array of image URLs). 
• 
Order: An Order object encapsulates a purchase made by a customer. Key 
fields are orderID, the customerID who placed it, order date/time, total 
amount, and status (e.g., “Pending”, “Preparing”, “Shipped”, “Delivered”, 
“Cancelled”). It also records the chosen delivery methods for each part of the 
order and an overall payment status (e.g., “Paid” since we simulate payment). 
An Order contains multiple products potentially from different sellers, so it 
has a relationship to an intermediate structure (OrderItem) described next. In 
object terms, Order may have a list of OrderItem entries or a mapping of seller 
-> list of OrderItems for that order. 
• 
OrderItem: Because a single order can include multiple products (even from 
different farmers), we define OrderItem as a line-item in an order. Each 
OrderItem links an Order to a specific Product and Farmer. Attributes are 
orderID, productID, quantity ordered, unit price at the time of order, and 
sellerID (which is redundant to get via product, but stored for quick access). 
We also calculate a subtotal for that line (quantity * price). This structure 
allows, for example, an Order with ID 101 to have two OrderItems: one for 
“Tomatoes from FarmerA, 5 kg at 10 TL/kg”, and one for “Honey from

FarmerB, 1 jar at 50 TL each”. It normalizes the many-to-many relationship 
between orders and products. When an order is created, we will generate one 
OrderItem per distinct product in the cart. This also makes it straightforward 
for each farmer to see their portion of an order (they can query OrderItems by 
sellerID). 
• 
Review (Evaluation): Represents a rating/comment given by a customer about 
either a product or a farmer. Attributes: reviewID, author (customerID), rating 
value (1–5), text comment, date, and a reference to what is being reviewed. For 
the reference, we might use fields like productID and farmerID, or a 
polymorphic reference. In our design, we include a field (e.g., reviewType) to 
indicate if it’s a product review or a farmer review, and then store the 
corresponding ID. For example, a product review would have 
reviewType=“product” and a productID set (farmerID may be null in that 
case, or derived from the product), whereas a farmer review would have 
reviewType=“farmer” and link directly to the farmer’s userID. We also include 
a moderation status (approved or deleted) so that if a review is removed by a 
moderator, it’s flagged accordingly (possibly not actually deleted from DB for 
record-keeping). 
• 
Payment [Simulation]: Since real payments aren’t processed, instead of a 
complex Payment entity, we will use a simple record to log simulated 
payment events. Fields might include paymentID, related orderID, payment 
date, method (e.g., “Online Test Card” vs “NFC Simulation”), and amount 
paid. Crucially, we also store the withholding tax amount for that order here 
as part of financial records. This Payment record ensures that even though no 
money moved, we have an audit trail of what would be paid to the farmer and 
what tax was taken. In future, if integrating real payments, this can be 
expanded or replaced with actual transaction references. 
• 
ChatMessage: (Optional logging for AI chatbot interactions.) If we decide to 
store chat logs for analysis, a ChatMessage class will hold messages 
exchanged in the support chatbot conversation. Fields: messageID, sessionID 
(to group messages by a conversation), sender (could be “user” or “bot”), 
message content, timestamp. This is not core to functionality, but it’s useful for 
reviewing how the chatbot is used and improving it. We design it such that 
storing these does not violate privacy – they will not contain personally 
identifiable info (the bot’s domain is limited to help topics).

General Operational Summary: Kırsof will operate as an online marketplace

Farmers sign up and list their agricultural products (produce, dairy, etc.) with

maintenance message, but such updates would typically be scheduled in off-peak

6. System Architecture and Preliminary Design 
6.1 System Architecture 
The Kırsof platform will be built with a modern multi-tier client–server architecture, 
separating front-end clients, back-end services, and database storage. On the client 
side, there will be two interfaces: a web application developed with React (for 
desktop and mobile browsers) and a mobile application developed with React 
Native (for Android and iOS). These clients will communicate over the network with 
a central Node.js back-end (using an Express.js framework) that exposes a RESTful 
API. The back-end will interact with a PostgreSQL database hosted on Supabase (a 
Backend-as-a-Service platform) which provides authentication, data storage, and file 
storage for the application. External libraries and services will also be integrated into 
this architecture – for example, the front-end will use Leaflet (with OpenStreetMap 
data) for interactive maps, and the mobile app will leverage the 
device’s NFC capabilities for simulating contactless payments. All components will 
be hosted in the cloud (using containerization for the Node.js server) to ensure the 
system is accessible anywhere and can scale as needed. 
  
This architecture is designed to be modular, flexible, and scalable. Each major 
function of the system is handled by a separate component: the React and React 
Native clients handle all user interface and user experience logic, the Node.js server 
contains the core business logic (e.g. processing orders, enforcing rules like tax 
withholding, etc.), and Supabase manages persistent data storage and some out-of-
the-box services (like user authentication and image storage). By following a client–
server model with clear separation of concerns, the team will ensure that changes in 
one part (for instance, updating the mobile UI or swapping out a service) will have 
minimal impact on other parts. The system will use standard interfaces between 
components – chiefly, HTTP requests with JSON payloads between front-ends and 
the Node.js API – making it easy to maintain and extend. In addition, the architecture 
will be designed with open standards in mind to avoid vendor lock-in. For example, 
using Leaflet with open map data instead of a proprietary maps API will avoid usage 
costs and allow switching to alternatives (like Mapbox) in the future if needed. 
Similarly, although the database is initially on Supabase, the code will use standard

content (the web app assets) can be served via a CDN to handle thousands of

9.4 Communication Interfaces 
The system will employ standard communication protocols and data exchange 
methods to ensure reliability and compatibility. The primary communication 
interface in Kırsof is the HTTP/HTTPS protocol used between the client applications 
(web browser or mobile app) and the back-end server. All network calls will be made 
over HTTPS to encrypt data in transit and protect sensitive information such as login 
credentials and personal details. The back-end will expose a set of RESTful API 
endpoints (e.g., /api/products, /api/orders, /api/login) that the clients can call to 
perform actions or retrieve data. These endpoints will follow REST conventions and 
use standard HTTP methods (GET for fetching data, POST for creating, PUT/PATCH 
for updating, DELETE for removing). Data exchanged through the API will be 
formatted in JSON, which is a lightweight and widely-used format easily handled by 
JavaScript on both client and server. For example, when the mobile app submits a 
new order, it will send a JSON payload to a POST endpoint, and the server will 
respond with a JSON confirmation containing the order details and status. This use 
of HTTP+JSON ensures that even if in the future other systems or third-party services

In terms of device-to-device communication, as mentioned in the hardware 
section, NFC communication is a special case of a communication interface. When 
two phones interact via NFC for the payment simulation, they use the NFC protocol 
which operates at very short range (a few centimeters). The technical standard likely 
used will be NDEF (NFC Data Exchange Format) for exchanging a small message. 
The team will define a simple NDEF message format (for instance, a text record that 
says something like “KIRSOF_PAY:{orderID}:{confirmationCode}”) that can be 
passed from one device to another. The mobile app will be programmed to recognize 
and parse this message to complete the payment confirmation process. This is a local, 
offline communication method, but it needs to be coordinated with the app’s logic – 
for example, once an NFC tap is detected and validated, the app will still probably 
communicate with the server over HTTPS to mark the order as paid. Thus, NFC 
serves as a trigger/interface between two devices, after which the normal client-
server communication continues. The use of NFC will adhere to the platform 
standards (Android’s and iOS’s guidelines for NFC use) to ensure compatibility and 
safety (the app will not read any sensitive data nor write anything permanent via 
NFC, only the intended small token). 
  
It’s worth noting that in this initial design, real-time communication (such as web 
sockets or server-sent events for live updates) is not implemented, due to complexity 
and time constraints. Instead, the system will rely on regular API calls and 
notifications to update the client. For example, if an order status changes, the server 
will send a push notification or the client will fetch the latest status when the user 
refreshes their orders page. This decision keeps the communication model simpler 
(pure REST/HTTP) at the cost of not having instantaneous in-app updates; it’s a 
conscious trade-off to focus on core features first. In the future, the architecture could 
introduce a WebSocket service or use Supabase’s real-time features if live updates 
become critical.

10. Expected Outcomes and Targeted Impact 
Expected Outcomes: By the end of this graduation project, the team expects to 
deliver a functioning proof-of-concept for the Kırsof digital marketplace that 
demonstrates the feasibility of connecting local farmers and consumers directly. The 
system will address the problem outlined in the introduction: small-scale farmers 
currently struggle with limited market access and reliance on intermediaries, 
resulting in reduced incomes and higher prices for consumers. Kırsof’s solution – a 
mobile and web platform enabling farm-to-table commerce – is anticipated to 
empower farmers by giving them a direct sales channel and to benefit consumers by 
providing fresher produce at competitive prices. The final deliverable will include 
a React web application and React Native mobile app through which users can 
register, create profiles, list agricultural products for sale, browse available products 
(including a map view to find nearby farms or pickup points), place orders, and go 
through a checkout process with a simulated payment. The Node.js backend and 
Supabase database will be fully implemented to handle these transactions, including 
special features like automatic tax withholding calculations on each sale (ensuring 
compliance with legal requirements even for informal producers). We expect the 
system to demonstrate all core marketplace functionality on a small scale – for 
example, a few dozen test users will be able to use Kırsof to carry out end-to-end 
transactions in a pilot scenario. By project’s end, we aim to have a stable
